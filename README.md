# Oil-Painting-of-Flowers---Reflection-Project
The Fissure of Flowers: A Painting Act of Traces and Vanishing
Introduction
The collaborative painting project by Yang Longhai杨龙海, Li Yixuan李宜轩 and Liu Yuanyi 刘媛依 is not a depiction of "flowers", but a spiritual archaeology taking place in the field of oil painting. The medium is oil paint, yet the subject has long collapsed into implosive force — they reject the tyranny of physical form, suspend precision, and reduce brushstrokes to a kind of abstract potential energy. Color blocks, those modulated lights and dusts, begin to infiltrate, transgress and converse with one another. Contours dissolve themselves before they even take shape, growing freely on the warp and weft of the canvas, just like life itself, refusing to be imprisoned by lines. With knives and brushes, they pursue not a single flower, but the ineffable aura that vibrates continuously in the air after the flower disintegrates — the nameless existence before and after being.
The Essence of Transience
Thus, what the canvas bears is no longer a "still life", but amber and epitaphs of time. From the blazing visual clamor of herbaceous peonies to the almost aphasic delicacy of daisies, every layering and scraping of pigment is a microscopic observation and freeze-frame of "transience". Flowers, an eternal metaphor in classical aesthetics (often simplified into an aesthetic or even kitsch symbol, or closely linked to femininity), are completely stripped of their decorative nature here. They become a fissure leading to the abyss of existence. Through this fissure, we witness not the solidification of beauty, but the scene where beauty unfolds: how an energy surges from nothingness, takes form, and then embarks on the long journey of farewell at the very peak of form. This process itself constitutes the most profound philosophy and poetry.
Artistic Philosophy and Reflection
Their work is therefore a two-way reflection. On the one hand, it guards against solidifying flowers into stylized symbols (whether the rigidification brought by the freehand brushwork of the East or the still-life tradition of the West); on the other hand, it avoids falling into the "nihilism of meaning" that pure formal experiments may lead to. Between "resemblance and non-resemblance", they have found a contemporary fulcrum — a non-figurative artistic conception constructed by layered pigments and spatial blank-leaving. Viewers are invited, or rather required, to penetrate the enchanting surface of colors and discern the geological movements lurking beneath: the inquiries into the essence of life, the solitude and eternity of the individual in the torrent of time, and the eternally unbridgeable yet longing-filled gap between humanity and nature.
Conclusion: A Philosophical Celebration
Ultimately, for those who love oil painting and nature, this series of works offers not a peaceful place for "appreciation", but a turbulent homeland for spiritual "dwelling". As Heidegger put it, it attempts to confront the alienation and fragmentation brought by modernity through "poetic" creation. Here, every gaze is a rethinking of being and nothingness, and the warm light spots accidentally embedded in the seemingly pure white coverage (or any dominant color background) may be a silent and grand tribute to life itself — fragile yet resilient, striving and forging ahead. This is no longer merely painting; it is a philosophical writing in visuals, a lasting celebration of vanishing.
